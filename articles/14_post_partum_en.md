# Postpartum: Recovery and Adaptation

The postpartum period, extending from delivery until approximately six to eight weeks after birth, is a major transition phase both physically and psychologically. Often called the "fourth trimester," this period deserves particular attention as it involves significant bodily, emotional, and family changes. This article guides you through the different aspects of postpartum recovery and offers advice to facilitate this adaptation.

## Physical Changes of Postpartum

### Uterine Recovery

After delivery, the uterus begins a process called "involution" to gradually return to its pre-pregnancy size and position:

- **Size**: Just after delivery, the uterus weighs about 1 kg. In one week, it decreases by half, and after six weeks, it generally returns to its normal size (about 60g).
- **Uterine contractions**: You will feel uterine contractions, sometimes called "afterpains," particularly during breastfeeding (oxytocin released during feeds stimulates these contractions). They are generally more intense in multiparous women.
- **Lochia**: These postpartum vaginal discharges contain blood, mucus, and uterine tissue. Initially heavy and red, they gradually become pinkish then yellowish and decrease in quantity. They can last 2 to 6 weeks.

### Perineal Recovery

The perineum undergoes significant stress during delivery:

- **Without tear or episiotomy**: The perineum may be temporarily distended and sensitive.
- **With tear or episiotomy**: Complete healing generally takes 3 to 4 weeks. Pain, pulling sensations, or discomfort may persist.
- **Swelling and bruising**: Common in the first days.

### Breasts and Lactation

Whether you choose to breastfeed or not, your breasts will experience significant changes:

- **Milk coming in**: Generally between the 2nd and 5th day after delivery.
- **If breastfeeding**: Breasts gradually adapt to baby's demand. The first weeks may involve engorgement, sensitive nipples, or latching difficulties.
- **If not breastfeeding**: Milk production generally stops in one to two weeks. Avoid stimulating the breasts and wear a suitable bra.

### Other Physical Changes

- **Weight**: Immediate postpartum weight loss is approximately 5 to 6 kg. Remaining weight loss is gradual and varies considerably.
- **Belly**: The abdomen remains distended for several weeks. Abdominal muscles may present a separation (diastasis recti).
- **Sweating**: Heavy night sweats are common, allowing the body to eliminate excess fluids.
- **Urinary system**: Temporary retention or urinary leaks may occur.
- **Bowel transit**: Constipation is common in the first days.

## Recovery After Cesarean

### Scar Care

- The horizontal scar (Pfannenstiel) measures about 15 cm, located just above the pubis.
- Staples or stitches are generally removed between the 5th and 7th day.
- Complete healing takes about 6 weeks, but the scar continues to refine for several months.

### Post-Operative Pain

- Pain at the incision site is normal and may persist several weeks.
- Appropriate pain relief will be prescribed.

### Progressive Recovery

- Early mobilization is encouraged (from the next day) to prevent complications like thrombosis.
- Avoid heavy lifting for 6 to 8 weeks.
- Driving is generally discouraged for 4 to 6 weeks.
- Sports activities should not resume without medical approval, generally not before 8 to 12 weeks.

## Emotional and Psychological Changes

### Baby Blues

- Affects 50 to 80% of new mothers
- Generally appears between the 3rd and 5th day after delivery
- Characterized by emotional hypersensitivity, easy crying, anxiety, irritability
- Spontaneously disappears within a few days without specific treatment
- Requires support, understanding, and rest

### Postpartum Depression

More serious than baby blues, it affects about 10 to 15% of mothers:

- **Symptoms**: Persistent sadness, loss of interest, excessive fatigue, sleep disorders, guilt or inadequacy feelings, dark thoughts, difficulty bonding with baby.
- **Treatment**: Requires professional care (psychotherapy and/or medication compatible with breastfeeding if necessary).
- **Important**: Postpartum depression is not a sign of weakness or poor mothering. It is a medical complication requiring care.

### Postpartum Anxiety

Often less recognized, it manifests as: excessive worries about baby's health or safety, intrusive thoughts, panic attacks, compulsive checking behaviors.

### Post-Traumatic Stress Related to Delivery

About 3 to 6% of women develop PTSD following a traumatic birth experience. Specialized psychological care is recommended.

## Essential Postpartum Care

### Perineal Care

- **Hygiene**: Wash the perineal area after each bathroom visit with warm water. Pat dry gently.
- **Pain relief**: Apply cold packs during the first 24-48 hours, take warm sitz baths, use prescribed analgesics.
- **With episiotomy or tear**: Avoid sitting for long periods, keep the area clean and dry.

### Managing Lochia

- Use sanitary pads (not tampons) and change regularly
- Monitor evolution (quantity, color, odor)
- Consult if bleeding increases again, if you expel large clots, or if lochia smell foul

### Breast Care

- **If breastfeeding**: Let a drop of milk dry on the nipple after feeding, change positions regularly, use clean nursing pads, apply purified lanolin if needed.
- **If not breastfeeding**: Wear a suitable and firm bra, avoid breast stimulation, apply cold compresses for discomfort.

### Rest and Recovery

- "Sleep when your baby sleeps"
- Accept help offered by those around you
- Limit visits in the first days
- Prioritize essential tasks

## Postpartum Nutrition

### General Principles

- Prioritize a varied and balanced diet
- Hydrate abundantly (at least 2 liters of water per day, more if breastfeeding)
- Don't start restrictive diets, even if you want to lose weight
- Eat to your hunger, listening to your body

### Important Nutrients

- **Protein**: Essential for healing and muscle recovery
- **Iron**: To prevent anemia, especially after significant blood loss
- **Calcium**: Particularly important if breastfeeding
- **Fiber**: To prevent constipation
- **Vitamins and minerals**: To support overall recovery

## Resuming Physical Activity

### When to Start?

- **Light exercises** (short walks, breathing, gentle perineal contractions): From the first days
- **Moderate exercises** (longer walks, swimming): After the postnatal visit (6-8 weeks)
- **Intense exercises** (running, impact sports): Generally not before 3-6 months

### Recommended Exercises

- **Perineal strengthening**: Essential to prevent incontinence
- **Adapted abdominal strengthening**: Avoid classic sit-ups, especially with diastasis
- **Gentle cardiovascular**: Walking with baby, swimming after lochia stops

## Sexuality After Delivery

### When to Resume Sexual Relations?

- Medical recommendation: wait for complete healing (generally 4-6 weeks)
- The postnatal visit verifies everything is well healed
- Desire and emotional availability are essential - there's no rush

### Changes Affecting Sexuality

- **Vaginal dryness**: Due to hormonal changes, especially during breastfeeding
- **Breast sensitivity**: Particularly during breastfeeding
- **Perineal changes**: Sensitivity, scars
- **Fatigue**: A major factor affecting libido

### Contraception

Fertility can return quickly, even during breastfeeding. Discuss contraception options with your doctor.

## Adaptation to Parenthood

### Common Challenges

- Sleep deprivation
- New responsibilities
- Changes in couple dynamics
- Loss of autonomy
- Doubts and questioning

### Adaptation Strategies

- Accept imperfection
- Establish priorities
- Share responsibilities
- Maintain communication
- Take care of yourself
- Connect with other parents

### Building Bonds with Your Baby

- Practice regular skin-to-skin contact
- Observe and learn to decode your baby's signals
- Talk, sing, and tell stories
- Accept that bonding can be gradual

## When to Consult a Professional?

### Concerning Physical Signs

- Fever above 38°C
- Increasing bleeding or large clots
- Foul-smelling lochia
- Intense abdominal pain
- Leg pain, redness, or swelling (thrombosis risk)
- Breast pain, redness, or hardening (possible mastitis)

### Concerning Psychological Signs

- Persistent sadness beyond two weeks
- Loss of interest in the baby
- Frightening or obsessive thoughts
- Suicidal ideas
- Paralyzing anxiety

## Conclusion

The postpartum period is a major transition deserving attention and kindness. Your body accomplished the extraordinary feat of creating and bringing a human being into the world - it needs time to recover. Simultaneously, you are learning a new role as parent.

Don't hesitate to ask for help. Remember there is no perfect parent, and difficulties you encounter are normal and shared by many others. Take care of yourself as much as your baby - your well-being is essential for caring for them in the best conditions.
