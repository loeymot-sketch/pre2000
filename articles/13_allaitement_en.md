# Breastfeeding: Tips for a Good Start

Breastfeeding is a natural process that offers many benefits for both baby and mother. However, although natural, breastfeeding is not always instinctive and may require learning. This article provides practical advice to help you start breastfeeding well and overcome common difficulties you might encounter.

## Benefits of Breastfeeding

### For the Baby

- **Optimal nutrition**: Breast milk contains all the nutrients your baby needs during the first six months of life, in ideal proportions.
- **Immune protection**: Rich in antibodies and immune cells, breast milk protects your baby against many infections and diseases.
- **Easy digestion**: Breast milk proteins are perfectly adapted to your baby's immature digestive system.
- **Reduced long-term risks**: Breastfed children have reduced risks of obesity, type 2 diabetes, allergies, and asthma.
- **Cognitive development**: Some studies suggest a link between breastfeeding and better cognitive development.

### For the Mother

- **Postpartum recovery**: Breastfeeding stimulates oxytocin production, which helps the uterus return to its normal size more quickly.
- **Emotional bond**: Breastfeeding moments foster a special bond between mother and baby.
- **Health benefits**: Breastfeeding is associated with reduced risk of breast cancer, ovarian cancer, and osteoporosis.
- **Practical aspect**: Breast milk is always available, at the right temperature, without any preparation needed.
- **Savings**: Breastfeeding saves the cost of infant formula and associated accessories.

## Preparing for Breastfeeding During Pregnancy

### Getting Informed

- Attend breastfeeding preparation sessions, often offered as part of birth preparation.
- Read reference books on breastfeeding based on recent scientific data.
- Exchange with women who have had a positive breastfeeding experience.

### Preparing Useful Equipment

Although breastfeeding requires little equipment, some accessories can be useful:
- 2 to 3 adapted nursing bras
- Washable or disposable nursing pads
- A nursing pillow for more comfort
- Purified lanolin cream to prevent or treat nipple cracks
- A breast pump (manual or electric) if you plan to be away regularly

### Identifying Available Resources

Identify in advance the people and structures that can support you:
- IBCLC certified lactation consultants
- Breastfeeding support associations
- Midwives specialized in breastfeeding
- Groups of breastfeeding mothers

## The First Days: Starting Breastfeeding Well

The first days are crucial for establishing effective and comfortable breastfeeding.

### The First Feed

Ideally, the first feed should take place within the hour following birth, if the health of mother and baby permits. This special moment, often called the "golden hour," presents several advantages:
- The newborn's sucking reflex is particularly vigorous
- Skin-to-skin contact promotes the baby's temperature regulation
- Early breast stimulation encourages milk production

### Colostrum: The First Milk

In the first days, your breasts produce colostrum, a yellowish, thick, and concentrated liquid:
- Rich in antibodies and immune factors
- Perfectly adapted to the newborn's needs and digestive capacity
- Produced in small quantities (a few milliliters per feed), but sufficient for the first days
- Has a laxative effect that helps the baby evacuate meconium (first stools)

Don't worry about the small quantity produced: your newborn's stomach is tiny (the size of a cherry on the first day).

### Milk Coming In

Milk typically comes in between the 2nd and 5th day after delivery. It is characterized by:
- An increase in the volume of milk produced
- Breasts that become heavier, firmer, sometimes painful
- A sensation of warmth in the breasts

To manage discomfort when milk comes in:
- Breastfeed your baby frequently
- Apply cold compresses between feeds
- Wear a suitable bra, but not too tight
- Take a warm shower if necessary

## Breastfeeding Positions

A good position is essential for comfortable and effective breastfeeding.

### Basic Principles for All Positions

Regardless of the position chosen, certain principles remain constant:
- Your back, shoulders, and arms should be well supported
- The baby's body should be aligned (ear, shoulder, and hip in a line)
- The baby should be turned toward you, tummy to tummy
- Their head should be slightly tilted back, not leaning forward
- Bring the baby to the breast, not the breast to the baby (avoid leaning toward them)

### The Cradle Hold (Classic Position)

- Sit comfortably with your back well supported
- Place your baby on your forearm, their head in the crook of your elbow
- Their tummy should be against yours
- Support their bottom with your hand
- Use the other hand to guide your breast to their mouth

### The Cross-Cradle Hold

Particularly suited for newborns and small babies:
- Hold your baby on the forearm opposite the breast they will feed from
- Support their neck and shoulders with your hand
- This position gives you better control of the baby's head, useful for beginners

### The Football (Rugby) Hold

Ideal after a cesarean or for mothers with large breasts:
- Place your baby beside you, under your arm
- Their body is held by your forearm, feet pointing backward
- Support their neck with your hand
- This position avoids any pressure on the cesarean scar

### The Side-Lying Position

Perfect for nighttime feeds or when you're tired:
- Lie on your side, with a pillow under your head and another behind your back
- Place your baby on their side, facing you, tummy to tummy
- You can support their back with your free arm or a small cushion

## Correct Latch

A good latch is crucial to avoid pain and ensure effective milk transfer.

### How to Get a Good Latch

1. **Position your baby correctly**: their nose should be at the level of your nipple
2. **Stimulate their lips**: lightly touch their lips with your nipple to encourage them to open wide
3. **Wait for them to open wide**: like a yawn, not just slightly open
4. **Bring them quickly to the breast**: when their mouth is wide open, firmly bring them toward your breast
5. **Make sure they take a good portion of the areola**: not just the nipple

### Signs of a Good Latch

- Baby's lips are flanged outward (like a suction cup)
- Their chin touches your breast
- Their nose is clear or barely touches the breast
- Their cheeks are rounded, not hollowed
- More areola visible above the upper lip than below
- You hear or see your baby swallowing
- Feeding is not painful after the first few sucks

### Signs of a Poor Latch

- Persistent pain throughout the feed
- Nipple deformed, flattened, or striped after feeding
- Audible tongue clicking
- Hollowed cheeks during sucking
- Baby frustrated, frequently releasing and re-latching

If you notice these signs, gently interrupt the feed by inserting your finger in the corner of the baby's mouth to break the seal, then start again.

## Feeding Rhythm

On-demand feeding is generally recommended, especially at the beginning.

### Feed Frequency

- A newborn typically feeds 8 to 12 times per 24 hours
- The interval between feeds can vary from 1 to 3 hours
- Some babies practice "cluster feeding" (feeds close together for a few hours, followed by a longer sleep period)

### Feed Duration

- Duration can vary considerably from one baby to another
- Let your baby feed as long as they want at the first breast
- Offer the second breast if they still seem hungry
- Start the next feed with the breast offered last or stimulated least

### Signs of Hunger

Learn to recognize early hunger signs:
- Sucking or licking movements
- Hand brought to mouth
- Rooting movements (turning head with mouth open)
- Small sounds
- Restlessness

Crying is a late sign of hunger. Ideally, offer the breast before your baby cries.

### Signs of Satiety

Your baby is probably satisfied if they:
- Spontaneously release the breast
- Fall deeply asleep
- Open their hands (which were often clenched during feeding)
- Seem relaxed and content

## Overcoming Common Difficulties

### Nipple Pain and Cracks

**Possible causes**: Poor latch, incorrect position, tongue tie, incorrect pump use

**Solutions**: Check and correct the latch, try different positions, apply breast milk drops on the nipple after feeding, use purified lanolin cream, air-dry nipples, change nursing pads regularly, consult if pain persists.

### Engorgement

**Possible causes**: Milk coming in, infrequent or ineffective feeds, too-tight clothing

**Solutions**: Feed frequently, ensure correct latch, apply cold compresses between feeds, apply warm compresses before feeding, express a little milk manually if breasts are very tense, gently massage during feeding.

### Blocked Milk Duct

**Signs**: Painful, red, and hard area in the breast; sometimes a white spot on the nipple

**Solutions**: Continue frequent feeding starting with the affected breast, position baby's chin facing the blocked area, apply heat before feeding, gently massage during feeding, vary positions, avoid tight clothing.

### Mastitis

**Signs**: Symptoms similar to a blocked duct but more intense, fever (>38.5°C), flu-like symptoms

**Solutions**: Consult a doctor quickly (antibiotics may be needed), continue frequent breastfeeding, rest as much as possible, drink plenty of water.

### Perceived Insufficient Milk Supply

**Signs your baby is getting enough milk**:
- 6 to 8 well-wet diapers per day
- 2 to 5 stools per day during the first month
- Steady weight gain (return to birth weight in 10-14 days, then 150-200g per week)
- Baby seems satisfied after most feeds

**To increase your milk supply**: Feed more frequently, ensure correct latch, offer both breasts each feed, avoid formula supplements unless medically indicated, stay well hydrated and fed, rest enough.

## Combining Breastfeeding and Returning to Work

### Preparing the Transition

- Start pumping about 2-3 weeks before returning
- Gradually build up a milk supply in the freezer
- Get your baby used to bottle or cup (depending on age)
- Learn about your rights (breastfeeding breaks, schedule adjustments)

### Options for Maintaining Breastfeeding

- **Mixed feeding**: Breastfeed when with your baby, use pumped milk or formula when absent
- **Continued exclusive breastfeeding**: Pump during work hours
- **Partial breastfeeding**: Maintain some feeds (morning, evening, night) and supplement with formula

### Practical Tips for Pumping at Work

- Inform your employer in advance
- Find a clean and private space
- Plan all necessary equipment (pump, containers, cooler)
- Establish a regular schedule
- Store pumped milk correctly (refrigerator or cooler with ice packs)

## Conclusion

Breastfeeding is a unique experience that evolves over time. The beginning can sometimes be difficult, but with good information, support, and perseverance, most difficulties can be overcome.

Don't hesitate to ask for help if you encounter problems. Lactation consultants, midwives, and breastfeeding support associations are there to accompany you on this adventure.

Remember that every breastfeeding experience is unique. What works for one mother may not work for another. The essential thing is to find what works best for you and your baby, in an atmosphere of kindness and without judgment.
