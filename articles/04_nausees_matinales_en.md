# Managing Morning Sickness: Tips and Remedies

Morning sickness is one of the most common symptoms of pregnancy, affecting about 70 to 80% of pregnant women. Despite its name, this sickness can occur at any time of the day and its intensity varies considerably from one woman to another. This article offers you practical tips and effective remedies to better manage this uncomfortable period.

## Understanding Pregnancy Sickness

Nausea and vomiting of pregnancy generally appear between the 4th and 6th week after the last period and often subside after the first trimester, around the 12th or 16th week. However, for some women, they can persist longer, sometimes even until childbirth.

Although their exact cause remains uncertain, several factors seem involved:
- The rapid increase in hCG hormone (human chorionic gonadotropin)
- Hormonal changes, particularly the rise in estrogen levels
- Increased sensitivity to smells
- Genetic factors (if your mother or sister suffered from severe sickness during their pregnancy, you are more likely to suffer from it too)
- Psychological factors like stress or anxiety

It is important to note that nausea, although unpleasant, is generally considered a sign of a healthy pregnancy. Studies have even shown that women who suffer from nausea have a lower risk of miscarriage. However, this does not mean that the absence of nausea indicates a problem.

## Dietary Strategies

Diet plays a crucial role in managing morning sickness. Here are some tips that can help you:

### Frequent Small Meals

Rather than three large meals, opt for 5 to 6 small meals spread throughout the day. A stomach that is too full or too empty can aggravate nausea.

### Eat Before Getting Up

Keep dry biscuits or crackers on your bedside table and eat a few before even getting up in the morning. Then stay lying down for about fifteen minutes to allow your digestion to start.

### Prioritize Certain Foods

Some foods are generally better tolerated:
- Protein-rich foods (eggs, poultry, fish)
- Starches (bread, rice, potatoes)
- Fresh fruits and vegetables
- Cold or room temperature foods, which release fewer odors

### Avoid Certain Foods

Conversely, some foods can aggravate nausea:
- Fatty, fried, or spicy foods
- Strong-smelling foods (fish, cabbage, garlic)
- Very sweet foods
- Very hot dishes, which release more odors

### Drink Enough, But Not During Meals

Hydration is essential, but drinking during meals can increase the feeling of fullness and aggravate nausea. Try to drink between meals, in small regular sips. Cold drinks, like lemon water or lukewarm ginger infusions, are often well tolerated.

## Natural Remedies

Several natural remedies can help relieve pregnancy nausea:

### Ginger

Ginger is one of the most studied and effective natural remedies against pregnancy nausea. You can consume it in different forms:
- Fresh ginger infusion (a few slices in hot water)
- Candied or crystallized ginger
- Ginger biscuits
- Ginger supplements (after medical advice)

### Acupressure

Stimulation of point P6 (or Neiguan), located on the inside of the wrist about three fingers above the wrist crease, can relieve nausea. You can stimulate this point yourself by pressing on it for a few minutes, or use acupressure wristbands specially designed for this purpose, available in pharmacies.

### Essential Oils

Some essential oils can help reduce nausea, notably:
- Lemon essential oil: a few drops to breathe in (never ingest)
- Peppermint essential oil: to breathe in only, avoiding contact with skin

Warning: the use of essential oils during pregnancy must always be done with caution and after medical advice, as some are contraindicated.

### Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine)

Studies have shown that vitamin B6 can help reduce pregnancy nausea. Talk to your doctor or midwife who can prescribe a suitable supplement if necessary.

## Lifestyle Adjustments

Some changes in your daily life can also contribute to reducing nausea:

### Avoid Triggers

Identify what triggers or aggravates your nausea (certain smells, fatigue, heat) and try to avoid them as much as possible.

### Get Enough Rest

Fatigue can aggravate nausea. Try to rest more, take naps if possible, and adopt good sleep hygiene.

### Get Fresh Air Regularly

Going out for fresh air can help reduce nausea. If possible, take short daily walks.

### Wear Comfortable Clothes

Avoid tight clothes around the waist or stomach, which can accentuate the feeling of nausea.

### Practice Relaxation Techniques

Stress can aggravate nausea. Techniques like deep breathing, meditation, or prenatal yoga can help you relax.

## When to Consult a Doctor?

In most cases, pregnancy nausea, although unpleasant, does not present a risk to the mother or the baby. However, it is important to consult a healthcare professional quickly in the following cases:

### Hyperemesis Gravidarum

This severe form of nausea and vomiting affects about 1 to 3% of pregnant women. It is characterized by:
- Frequent vomiting (more than 3 to 4 times a day)
- Inability to keep down food and fluids
- Weight loss of more than 5% of initial weight
- Signs of dehydration (intense thirst, dark urine, dizziness)
- Extreme fatigue

Hyperemesis gravidarum requires medical management, sometimes hospitalization for rehydration via intravenous line and medication monitoring.

### Other Warning Signs

Also consult if you have:
- Intense abdominal pain
- Fever
- Bloody vomiting or vomiting that looks like coffee grounds
- Severe headaches or vision problems

## Medication Treatments

In case of severe nausea, your doctor may prescribe antiemetic medications suitable for pregnancy. These medicines are generally prescribed after diet measures and natural remedies have failed, or straight away in case of hyperemesis gravidarum.

Several medications have demonstrated their safety during pregnancy, particularly doxylamine-pyridoxine, metoclopramide or ondansetron in certain cases. These treatments must never be taken as self-medication and require a medical prescription.

## Conclusion

Pregnancy nausea, although unpleasant, is generally temporary and safe for your baby. By adopting a few dietary and lifestyle adjustments, and using certain natural remedies, you can often relieve them significantly.

Do not hesitate to talk about your nausea to your doctor or midwife during your prenatal consultations. They can offer solutions adapted to your situation and ensure that these symptoms do not affect your health or that of your baby.

Remember that each pregnancy is unique, and what works for one woman may not work for another. Be patient and try different approaches until you find the one that suits you best.
