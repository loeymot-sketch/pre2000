# Pregnancy Symptoms: How to Recognize Them

Pregnancy is a time of great change for a woman's body. Recognizing the first signs of pregnancy can sometimes be difficult, as some symptoms resemble those of premenstrual syndrome. This article will help you identify the most common symptoms of pregnancy and understand when to consult a healthcare professional.

## Missed Period: The Most Obvious Sign

Amenorrhea, or missed period, is generally the first sign that alerts women to a possible pregnancy. If you have regular menstrual cycles and notice a delay of more than a week, it is recommended to take a pregnancy test. However, not all women have regular cycles, and some may even have slight bleeding during the first months of pregnancy, which can be confusing.

## Morning Sickness: Not Just in the Morning

Contrary to their name, so-called "morning" sickness can occur at any time of the day. They usually appear between the 4th and 6th week of pregnancy and often subside after the first trimester. These nausea can be accompanied by vomiting or not, and their intensity varies considerably from one woman to another. Some women do not suffer from it at all, while others can be seriously inconvenienced.

## Intense Fatigue: An Early Sign

Unusual and intense fatigue is often one of the first symptoms of pregnancy. This fatigue is due to hormonal changes, in particular the increase in progesterone levels, as well as the adaptation of the body to its new state. It is particularly marked during the first trimester, then tends to improve in the second trimester, before sometimes reappearing at the end of pregnancy.

## Sensitive and Painful Breasts

From the first weeks of pregnancy, the breasts can become more sensitive, swollen, and painful to the touch. The areolas may widen and darken. These changes are due to the increase in hormones and the preparation of the mammary glands for breastfeeding. This symptom can look like what some women feel before their periods, but with an often more marked intensity.

## Frequent Urges to Urinate

The increase in blood volume and the pressure exerted by the uterus on the bladder lead to more frequent urges to urinate. This symptom generally appears from the first weeks of pregnancy, subsides in the second trimester when the uterus rises into the abdomen, then reappears at the end of pregnancy when the baby descends into the pelvis.

## Changes in Appetite and Food Cravings

Particular food cravings or aversion to certain foods are classic symptoms of pregnancy. You might suddenly hate the coffee you loved or have an irresistible craving for strawberries in the middle of winter. These changes are linked to hormonal changes and can evolve throughout pregnancy.

## Mood Swings

Hormonal fluctuations can cause significant mood swings. You could go from laughter to tears in a few minutes without apparent reason. These emotional changes are completely normal during pregnancy and are mainly due to variations in estrogen and progesterone levels.

## Headaches

Some women suffer from more frequent headaches during pregnancy, particularly in the first trimester. These headaches are generally linked to hormonal changes, increased blood volume, and sometimes fatigue or stress.

## Dizziness and Lightheadedness

The drop in blood pressure, common in early pregnancy, can cause dizziness or lightheadedness, especially when changing positions quickly. These symptoms can be accentuated by fatigue or hunger.

## Constipation

Hormonal changes slow down intestinal transit, which can lead to constipation. This symptom can appear from the beginning of pregnancy and persist until childbirth.

## When to Take a Pregnancy Test?

If you have several of these symptoms, especially if you have a missed period, it is recommended to take a pregnancy test. Urine tests sold in pharmacies are generally reliable from the first day of missed period. For definitive confirmation, consult your doctor or midwife who can prescribe a blood test to measure the beta-hCG hormone, specific to pregnancy.

## When to Consult a Healthcare Professional?

If your pregnancy test is positive, make an appointment with a healthcare professional (general practitioner, gynecologist, or midwife) to confirm the pregnancy and start prenatal care. This first consultation is generally scheduled between the 6th and 8th week of amenorrhea.

Consult quickly in case of intense abdominal pain, heavy bleeding, severe vomiting preventing any food intake, fever, or any other worrying symptom.

## Conclusion

Every woman experiences her pregnancy differently and may present all or part of these symptoms, with varying intensity. Some women have very few signs, while others can be strongly inconvenienced. If you think you are pregnant, do not hesitate to take a pregnancy test and consult a healthcare professional for appropriate follow-up. Pregnancy is a wonderful time but requires special attention to ensure your well-being and that of your future baby.
