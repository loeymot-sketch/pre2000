# Twins and Multiple Pregnancies: Specifics and Advice

A multiple pregnancy, whether twins, triplets, or more, is an extraordinary adventure accompanied by particular challenges. This article presents the specifics of multiple pregnancies, from diagnosis to preparations for the arrival of several babies, including adapted medical follow-up and practical advice for experiencing this journey serenely.

## Different Types of Multiple Pregnancies

### Monozygotic Twins (Identical)

Monozygotic twins come from a single fertilized egg that splits into two distinct embryos:
- They share the same genetic heritage
- They are always of the same sex
- They can look very similar, but develop distinct personalities
- Represent about 1/3 of twin pregnancies

Depending on when the egg splits, monozygotic twins may or may not share:
- The placenta (monochorionic or dichorionic)
- The amniotic sac (monoamniotic or biamniotic)

These distinctions are important as they determine certain specific risks and influence medical follow-up.

### Dizygotic Twins (Fraternal)

Dizygotic twins result from the fertilization of two different eggs by two distinct sperm cells:
- They share on average 50% of their genetic heritage, like siblings born at different times
- They can be of different sexes
- Each has their own placenta and own amniotic sac
- Represent about 2/3 of twin pregnancies

### Factors Favoring Multiple Pregnancies

Several factors increase the probability of a multiple pregnancy:
- **Genetic factors**: Family predisposition to dizygotic twins (on the maternal side)
- **Maternal age**: Women over 35 naturally have higher chances of having twins
- **Medically assisted reproduction**: Fertility treatments significantly increase the risk of multiple pregnancy
- **Ethnicity**: Some populations have higher rates of multiple births
- **Parity**: Women who have already had several children have higher chances of having twins

## Diagnosing a Multiple Pregnancy

### Suggestive Signs

Some signs may raise suspicion of a multiple pregnancy even before ultrasound confirmation:
- Particularly high HCG hormone levels
- More intense pregnancy symptoms (nausea, fatigue, sore breasts)
- Faster weight gain than average
- Uterus larger than expected for the term

However, only an ultrasound can confirm a multiple pregnancy with certainty.

### Ultrasound Confirmation

- **Early ultrasound** (6-8 weeks): Allows visualization of the number of gestational sacs and embryos
- **First trimester ultrasound** (11-13 weeks): Confirms the type of twinning (mono or dizygotic) by observing the membranes separating the fetuses
- **Determination of chorionicity**: Essential for assessing risks and adapting follow-up

## Specific Medical Follow-up

### Frequency of Consultations

Follow-up for a multiple pregnancy is closer than for a single pregnancy:
- Monthly consultations until 24 weeks
- Bi-monthly consultations from 24 to 32 weeks
- Weekly consultations after 32 weeks

### Additional Ultrasounds

- **Standard ultrasounds**: The three mandatory ultrasounds are performed as for a single pregnancy
- **Additional ultrasounds**: Generally one per month to monitor fetal growth
- **Specific ultrasounds** for monochorionic pregnancies: Every two weeks from 16 weeks to screen for twin-to-twin transfusion syndrome

### Specific Screenings and Exams

- **Trisomy 21 screening**: Adapted for multiple pregnancies
- **Gestational diabetes screening**: Earlier, generally from 24 weeks
- **Blood pressure monitoring**: More frequent due to increased risk of preeclampsia
- **Cervical monitoring**: Regular ultrasound measurements to prevent premature birth

### Nutritional Follow-up

- **Increased caloric needs**: About 300 extra calories per fetus per day
- **Adapted supplementation**: Iron, folic acid, calcium in larger quantities
- **Recommended weight gain**:
  * For normal weight women: 16 to 20 kg (vs 11 to 16 kg for a single pregnancy)

## Specific Risks of Multiple Pregnancies

### For the Mother

- **Hyperemesis gravidarum**: Severe nausea and vomiting
- **Anemia**: More frequent due to increased iron needs
- **Gestational diabetes**: 2 to 3 times more frequent
- **High blood pressure and preeclampsia**: Risk multiplied by 2 to 3
- **Postpartum hemorrhage**: Due to significant uterine distention
- **Cesarean section**: More frequent

### For the Babies

- **Prematurity**: Main risk of multiple pregnancies
  * Twins: 60% born before 37 weeks
  * Triplets: 90% born before 35 weeks
- **Intrauterine growth restriction**: Frequent, especially after 28-30 weeks
- **Twin-to-Twin Transfusion Syndrome**: Specific risk for monochorionic pregnancies (10-15%)

## Living a Multiple Pregnancy Daily

### Physical Adaptation

#### Managing Intensified Symptoms
- **Nausea**: Split meals, avoid empty stomach, ginger
- **Fatigue**: Frequent rest, naps, delegating tasks
- **Back pain**: Support belt, adapted exercises, osteopathy

#### Adapted Physical Activity
- **Recommended exercises**: Walking, swimming, prenatal yoga special for multiple pregnancy
- **To avoid**: Impact sports, lying flat on back after first trimester

### Psychological Preparation

#### Accepting the News
- Announcement can cause shock, even if desired
- Phases of adaptation: surprise, worry, acceptance, projection
- Importance of expressing emotions, even ambivalent ones

#### Preparing for Multiple Parenthood
- Learn about specifics of raising multiples
- Meet other parents of twins/triplets
- Think ahead about practical organization

### Practical Organization

#### Home Setup
- Space optimization: plan space for two cribs, two bouncers, etc.
- Functional organization: changing station, feeding area
- Storage: plan space for double supplies

#### Material Preparation
- **Essential double purchases**: Cribs, car seats
- **Single purchases**: Changing table, bathtub
- **Economy strategies**: Renting, second-hand, loans

## Delivering Twins

### Method of Delivery

Depends on several factors:
- **Presentation of babies**: Ideally both cephalic (head down)
- **Type of twinning**: Monoamniotic almost always via C-section
- **Term**: Earlier term increases C-section frequency

#### Vaginal Delivery
- Possible if first twin is head down
- Second twin can be breech in some cases
- Requires experienced team

#### Scheduled Cesarean
- Systematic for certain situations (monoamniotic, first twin breech)
- Often preferred for triplets and more
- Generally performed between 36 and 38 weeks for twins

## The First Months with Twins

### Breastfeeding Twins

#### Breastfeeding
- **Totally possible**: Milk production adapts to demand
- **Specific positions**: Double football hold, cross-cradle
- **Organization**: Feed together or separately
- **Support necessary**: Lactation consultant specialized in multiples

#### Bottle Feeding
- **Practical organization**: Preparing bottles in advance
- **Partner involvement**: Equitable sharing of feeds

### Sleep

- **Shared or separate room?** Both options possible
- **Synchronizing rhythms**: Waking the second when the first wakes to feed?
- **Night organization**: Relay system between parents

### Relationship with Each Child

- **Individualization**: Importance of considering each child as unique
- **Avoid constant comparisons**
- **Privileged moments** one-on-one with each baby

## Conclusion

A multiple pregnancy is a unique experience that involves special challenges but also multiplied joys. Although more demanding medically and organizationally than a single pregnancy, it can be experienced serenely with adapted follow-up and good preparation.

Reinforced medical accompaniment, support from those around you, and connecting with other parents of multiples are precious assets for navigating this adventure.
