# Essential Medical Exams During Pregnancy

Medical follow-up during pregnancy is fundamental to ensure your health and that of your baby. A series of medical exams is scheduled throughout these nine months to monitor the smooth progress of the pregnancy and detect possible complications. This article introduces you to the different recommended exams, their procedure, and their importance.

## The First Prenatal Consultation

Ideally, this consultation should take place before the end of the first trimester, usually between the 6th and 8th week of amenorrhea (absence of periods). This first visit is particularly important as it allows to:

- Confirm the pregnancy and date its beginning
- Establish your complete medical file (personal and family history)
- Perform a general clinical examination (weight, height, blood pressure)
- Prescribe the first biological exams (blood type, rhesus, irregular antibody screening, toxoplasmosis, rubella, syphilis, hepatitis B, HIV serologies)
- Evaluate possible risk factors
- Inform you about the follow-up schedule and rights related to maternity
- Prescribe necessary vitamin supplements, especially folic acid

During this consultation, the doctor or midwife will also calculate your estimated due date, which will serve as a reference throughout your pregnancy.

## Monthly Follow-up Consultations

In France, seven prenatal consultations are mandatory for a normal pregnancy, one per month starting from the 4th month. Each consultation includes:

- An interview to improved on your health status and answer your questions
- Measurement of your weight and blood pressure
- A clinical and obstetrical examination
- Screening for protein and sugar in urine
- Listening to the baby's heartbeat
- Measurement of uterine height (from the 4th month)

These regular consultations allow monitoring the evolution of your pregnancy and early detection of possible complications such as high blood pressure, gestational diabetes, or fetal growth retardation.

## Early Prenatal Interview

Proposed in the 4th month of pregnancy, this individual or couple interview is not a medical exam strictly speaking, but a privileged time of exchange with a midwife. Lasting about 45 minutes to an hour, it allows you to discuss your expectations, needs, worries, and inform you about available resources during pregnancy and after birth.

## Obstetrical Ultrasounds

Three ultrasounds are recommended during a normal pregnancy:

### The First Ultrasound (12 weeks of amenorrhea)

This first-trimester ultrasound allows to:
- Confirm the pregnancy start date
- Check the number of embryos
- Measure nuchal translucency (screening for Down syndrome)
- Check the general morphology of the embryo
- Observe embryonic vitality

### The Second Ultrasound (22 weeks of amenorrhea)

This morphological ultrasound of the second trimester is particularly important as it allows a detailed study of the fetus's anatomy:
- In-depth examination of organs (heart, kidneys, brain, etc.)
- Verification of fetal growth
- Localization of the placenta
- Evaluation of the amount of amniotic fluid

### The Third Ultrasound (32 weeks of amenorrhea)

This last ultrasound of the third trimester allows to:
- Check the growth and vitality of the fetus
- Confirm the baby's presentation (head down or breech)
- Evaluate the amount of amniotic fluid
- Locate the placenta and check its functioning
- Estimate the baby's weight

## Prenatal Screening Exams

### Screening for Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)

This screening, offered to all pregnant women, is not mandatory. It includes:

- In the first trimester: a blood test to measure maternal serum markers (PAPP-A and free β-hCG) combined with the measurement of nuchal translucency during the first-trimester ultrasound.
- Calculation of a personalized risk based on these results and maternal age.

If the risk is considered high (greater than 1/1000), a cell-free DNA test (NIPT) may be proposed. This test, performed by a simple blood draw, analyzes fetal DNA present in maternal blood and offers reliability greater than 99%.

In case of a positive NIPT result or if you have specific risk factors, a definitive diagnosis by amniocentesis or chorionic villus sampling may be proposed. These invasive exams carry a low risk of miscarriage (about 0.5%).

### Screening for Gestational Diabetes

Generally performed between the 24th and 28th week of pregnancy, this screening consists of an oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT). After a fasting blood draw, you will have to drink a sugary solution, then blood samples will be taken at regular intervals to measure your blood sugar.

## Biological Exams

Throughout pregnancy, several blood and urine tests are prescribed:

### In the First Trimester
- Determination of blood type and rhesus if unknown
- Irregular antibody screening
- Complete blood count (CBC)
- Serologies (toxoplasmosis, rubella, syphilis, hepatitis B, HIV)
- Fasting blood sugar

### In the Second Trimester
- Control of toxoplasmosis serology (if negative in the first trimester)
- Irregular antibody screening
- Screening for gestational diabetes

### In the Third Trimester
- Control of toxoplasmosis serology (if still negative)
- Irregular antibody screening
- Vaginal swab for Group B Streptococcus (between 35 and 38 weeks)

## Complementary Exams

Depending on your medical situation or the evolution of your pregnancy, other exams may be prescribed:

### Fetal Monitoring

Also called cardiotocography, this exam simultaneously records the fetus's heart rate and uterine contractions. It is systematically performed at the end of pregnancy and in case of overdue term.

### Amniocentesis

This exam involves taking amniotic fluid through the abdominal wall under ultrasound control. It allows analyzing the fetus's chromosomes and diagnosing certain genetic anomalies with certainty.

### Doppler

This ultrasound exam studies blood circulation in the fetus's vessels, umbilical cord, and placenta. It is prescribed in case of suspected intrauterine growth retardation or other complications.

## Preparation for Birth and Parenthood

Although not a medical exam, birth preparation sessions are an integral part of pregnancy monitoring. Reimbursed by Health Insurance up to eight sessions, they allow you to:
- Inform yourself about the course of pregnancy, childbirth, and the postpartum period
- Learn breathing and relaxation techniques
- Prepare your body for childbirth
- Exchange with other expectant mothers
- Address questions related to newborn care and breastfeeding

## The 9th Month Consultation

This last consultation before childbirth is particularly important. It allows to:
- Verify that everything is normal for childbirth
- Discuss your birth plan
- Inform you about the warning signs of labor
- Plan a possible induction if necessary
- Answer your last questions

## Conclusion

Medical follow-up during pregnancy is essential to ensure your well-being and that of your baby. These regular exams allow monitoring the smooth progress of the pregnancy and intervening quickly in case of problems. Do not hesitate to ask all your questions to the healthcare professionals accompanying you and report any unusual symptoms between two consultations. Remember that this follow-up is adapted to each woman and can be reinforced according to your personal medical situation.
