# Signs of Labor: When to Go to the Maternity Ward

The approach of delivery is often a period of excitement but also anxiety for expectant mothers. One question comes up frequently: how to recognize the true signs of labor and when should you go to the maternity ward? This article will help you identify the different signs announcing delivery and give you clear benchmarks to know when it's time to head to the maternity ward.

## Precursor Signs of Delivery

Before the start of true labor, your body may present several warning signs in the days or weeks preceding delivery. These signs don't mean that delivery is imminent, but that it is being prepared.

### Baby's Descent (or "Engagement")

A few weeks before delivery (often 2 to 4 weeks for a first baby, sometimes only a few days for subsequent ones), the baby descends into the pelvis. This phenomenon, called "engagement," results in:

- A feeling of lightness at the top of the abdomen and rib cage
- Easier breathing
- Greater pressure on the lower belly and perineum
- More frequent urges to urinate
- Sometimes, a more "waddling" gait

### Braxton Hicks Contractions

These preparatory contractions, sometimes called "practice contractions," are characterized by:
- A temporary hardening of the uterus
- An absence of real pain (sensation of tightening rather than pain)
- Irregularity in their frequency and duration
- Disappearance when changing position or walking

These contractions gradually prepare the cervix for delivery but do not mean that labor has begun.

### Loss of the Mucus Plug

The mucus plug is a gelatinous substance that blocks the cervix during pregnancy to protect it from infections. Its loss manifests as:
- Thick, gelatinous vaginal discharge
- Clear, whitish color or slightly tinged with blood (then called "bloody show")

The loss of the mucus plug can occur several days or even weeks before delivery, or just before the start of labor. On its own, it does not indicate that delivery is imminent.

### "Intestinal Cleansing"

Many women notice accelerated intestinal transit shortly before delivery, resulting in:
- Softer or diarrheal stools
- Episodes of diarrhea without apparent cause
- Intestinal cramps

This phenomenon is due to the release of prostaglandins that prepare the body for delivery by stimulating uterine contractions, but which also act on the intestines.

### Energy Surge

Some women feel a sudden burst of energy a few days before delivery, sometimes called "nesting syndrome." They may feel driven to:
- Clean the house intensively
- Reorganize the baby's room
- Complete the last preparations

Although this is not a systematic sign, this phenomenon is quite common and could be related to hormonal changes preparing the body for delivery.

## Signs of True Labor

Unlike precursor signs, the following signs generally indicate that labor has truly started or is about to start.

### Regular and Painful Contractions

True labor contractions differ from Braxton Hicks contractions in several characteristics:

- **Regularity**: They occur at increasingly regular intervals
- **Increasing Intensity**: They progressively become stronger and more painful
- **Persistence**: They do not disappear with rest or change of position
- **Location**: Pain often starts in the lower back and radiates to the front of the belly
- **Duration**: Each contraction generally lasts between 30 seconds and 1 minute

At the beginning of labor, contractions may be spaced 10 to 20 minutes apart. As labor progresses, they get closer together to reach an interval of 2 to 3 minutes.

### Rupture of the Amniotic Sac

The rupture of the amniotic membranes, commonly called "water breaking," can manifest in different ways:

- **Abundant Flow**: A sudden and significant rush of fluid
- **Progressive Flow**: A slower and continuous trickle
- **Seeping**: Small leaks that can be confused with urinary leakage

The amniotic fluid is generally clear and odorless, with sometimes a slight pale yellow tint. If the fluid is greenish or brownish, this may indicate the presence of meconium (baby's first stools) and requires rapid medical evaluation.

Water breaking can occur:
- Before contractions begin (about 10-15% of cases)
- During labor
- Sometimes, it must be done artificially by the doctor or midwife (amniotomy)

### Cervical Changes

Although you cannot observe this sign yourself, cervical changes are an important indicator of active labor:
- **Effacement**: The cervix, normally about 3-4 cm long, gradually thins out
- **Dilation**: The cervical opening gradually widens to reach 10 cm

These changes are evaluated during a vaginal examination by a healthcare professional.

## When to Go to the Maternity Ward?

The decision to go to the maternity ward depends on several factors, including your parity (first baby or not), the distance between your home and the maternity ward, and specific recommendations from your medical team.

### For a First Baby

In general, it is recommended to go to the maternity ward when:
- Contractions are regular, painful, and occurring every 5 minutes for at least 1 hour
- Your water has broken, even in the absence of contractions
- You feel the need for pain relief (epidural)

Labor for a first delivery generally lasts longer (12 to 24 hours on average), so there is no need to rush at the first contractions.

### For a Second Baby or More

For subsequent deliveries, labor is often faster. It is therefore advisable to leave earlier:
- As soon as contractions are regular and painful, occurring every 7-10 minutes
- Immediately after water breaking

### Other Factors to Consider

- **Distance to the maternity ward**: If you live far away or traffic conditions are difficult, leave earlier
- **History of rapid deliveries**: If you have already had a quick delivery, don't delay
- **Weather conditions**: In case of bad weather, allow more time for the trip
- **Time of day**: Consider rush hours if you live in an urban area

### Special Cases Requiring Immediate Departure

Certain situations require going immediately to the maternity ward, regardless of contraction frequency:

- **Heavy vaginal bleeding** (more than just a "bloody show")
- **Decrease or absence of fetal movements**
- **Intense and continuous abdominal pain** (different from contractions that come and go)
- **Greenish or brownish amniotic fluid** after water breaking
- **Fever** (temperature above 38°C)
- **Severe headaches or visual disturbances** (possible signs of preeclampsia)
- **Very painful contractions before 37 weeks** (risk of premature delivery)

## How to Prepare for Departure to the Maternity Ward

To avoid last-minute stress, some preparations can be useful:

### The Maternity Bag

Prepare your bag about one month before the expected due date. It should contain:
- Your administrative and medical documents (ID, health insurance card, blood type card, pregnancy follow-up file)
- Comfortable clothes for the stay
- Clothes for the baby
- Toiletry items
- Breastfeeding accessories if you plan to breastfeed

### The Trip to the Maternity Ward

- Scout the route in advance and identify several alternative itineraries
- Plan where you will park your vehicle
- If you don't have a car, organize in advance how you will get to the maternity ward (taxi, friend, family)
- Keep important phone numbers easily accessible

### Last Checks Before Departure

- Notify the person who will accompany you for delivery
- Make sure your phone is charged
- Verify that you have all your important documents
- If you have other children, confirm childcare arrangements
- Close faucets, windows, and your home's door

## False Labor vs True Labor: How to Tell the Difference?

It can sometimes be difficult to distinguish true labor from false labor, especially for a first delivery.

### Characteristics of False Labor

- Irregular contractions that don't progressively get closer together
- Pain mainly located in the front of the belly
- Contractions that weaken or disappear with rest, walking, or a warm bath
- No cervical changes on examination (if you consult)

### Characteristics of True Labor

- Contractions that progressively become more regular, longer, and more painful
- Pain that often starts in the lower back and radiates forward
- Contractions that persist despite changes in position or activity
- Cervical changes (effacement and dilation) on examination

### What to Do in Case of Doubt?

- Time your contractions: note the start and end time of each contraction for one hour
- Change position or take a warm bath to see if contractions diminish
- Drink a large glass of water and lie on your left side for one hour
- Call the maternity ward or your midwife to describe your symptoms and receive personalized advice

Don't hesitate to consult in case of persistent doubt. It is better to go for nothing than to risk an unplanned home delivery.

## Conclusion

Recognizing the signs of labor and knowing when to go to the maternity ward may seem intimidating, but with good preparation and knowledge of the signals to watch for, you will be able to approach this stage with more serenity.

Remember that every woman and every delivery is unique. Some women experience all the precursor signs, others very few. Some labors begin gradually, others more suddenly.

In case of doubt, never hesitate to contact your maternity ward or your midwife. These professionals are there to guide and reassure you during this important stage of your journey toward parenthood.
